Question----
In a variant of Monty Hall, Monty does not always follow the same strategy. Sometimes he prefers one door over another, and sometimes his behavior depends on random choices. After observing which door Monty opens, determine whether switching is still optimal.

##My First Instinct
My first instinct was that if the same door gets opened, the answer should be the same as the classic Monty Hall problem.
I was focusing on what door was opened and not why it was opened.

##What Assumption Was Missing?
I ignored Monty's decision process.
The probability of seeing a particular door opened depends on where the car actually is. Some worlds make Monty's action more likely than others.
The evidence is not just the opened door. The evidence is the opened door plus the mechanism that produced that observation.

##Correct Model
For each possible car location:
Assign a prior probability.
Compute the probability that Monty would make the observed move.
Multiply prior × likelihood.
Normalize.
The worlds where Monty's action is more likely gain posterior weight.

##Takeaway
Evidence cannot be separated from the process that generated it.
Two observations that look identical can imply different probabilities if they were generated by different rules.

##Real-World Connection
Medical tests, spam filters, market signals, and interviews all work the same way. The meaning of evidence depends on how that evidence was produced.
